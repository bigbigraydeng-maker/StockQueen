Insider Money Doesn't Lie: How We Extract CEO Buying Signals from Free SEC Data

March 22, 2026 · StockQueen Research · 12 min read · Event-Driven Insider Signals SEC EDGAR Free Data
阅读中文版 →

Imagine someone who knows a company inside out — the product roadmap, next quarter's order book, what management really thinks about the next 12 months — and then puts their own personal cash into that company's stock on the open market. That signal is more direct than any analyst report. In the US, that information is publicly available and completely free.

Form 4: The Free Dataset Nobody Bothers to Clean

US securities law requires corporate insiders — CEOs, CFOs, directors, and other officers — to file a Form 4 with the SEC within two business days of any transaction in their company's stock. The filing discloses what was traded, how many shares, at what price, and when. All of this flows into SEC EDGAR in real time, fully structured, completely free.

Hundreds of Form 4 filings hit EDGAR every trading day. Quant funds have been watching this data for years — so is there still an edge? Yes, but only if you're willing to do the unglamorous work of filtering out the noise. The filter is the moat.

Core insight: The value isn't in knowing that someone bought. It's in knowing who bought, how much, and whether multiple insiders moved together. Raw Form 4 data is 90% noise — options exercises, scheduled plan acquisitions, gift transfers. Open-market buys with real signal content are a small fraction.

Twenty years of research on insider trading signals produces remarkably consistent conclusions:

Buy signals dominate sell signals — and for good reason. An insider might sell because they need cash, because options are expiring, because they're diversifying their wealth, or for tax planning. But nobody buys their own stock on the open market when they think the price is going down. The asymmetry in intent is fundamental.

Why Most Insider Signal Tools Fail

The data quality problem with Form 4 is an open secret. Here are the three biggest noise sources:

M

Option Exercises (Transaction Code = M)

The single largest noise source. When executives exercise options, they're executing a compensation plan, not expressing a view on the stock. But raw data treats these identically to open-market buys.

A/G

Plan Acquisitions and Gifts (Code = A / G)

RSU vestings, ESPP contributions, charitable transfers. Scheduled and automatic — they carry zero informational content. Yet they flood the raw dataset with apparent "buy" signals.

10%

10% Shareholders Who Aren't Management

Large external shareholders also file Form 4. They may be activist investors or funds with no particular information advantage about the company's operations. Needs to be distinguished from true management insiders.

Using raw Form 4 data without filtering almost certainly produces negative alpha. The cleaning process itself is the defensible edge.

Our Cleaning Pipeline: Two-Layer Filtering

The StockQueen Form 4 pipeline applies two sequential filtering layers, each with explicit justification:

Layer 1: XML Parsing

1
Keep only P (open-market purchase) and S (open-market sale)

Drops M, A, G, F, and all other non-market transaction codes. Only open-market transactions represent voluntary informed conviction.

2
Price > 0 (drop zero-price filings)

Genuine open-market trades always have a price. Missing price = plan transfer or grant.

3
Notional value ≥ $50,000

Filters out token purchases that signal nothing. If a CEO buys $500, it tells you nothing about their conviction.

Layer 2: Role Filter

4
Must be officer OR director

Excludes pure 10% shareholders who are not management. External large shareholders have a different — and typically weaker — informational position.

After these four filters, the transactions with genuine signal value represent roughly 5–10% of all Form 4 filings on any given day. Less is more — this is exactly why the factor is difficult to replicate.

Signal Tiering: Not All Buys Are Equal

Cleaned transactions are then aggregated into one of six signal tiers, written to the shared event_signals table:

— Cluster Sell 3+ distinct insiders sell within 5 days 0.40

Sell signals are deliberately weighted low. The multi-motive problem for selling makes it far less actionable than buying.

Technical Implementation: EDGAR's Rate Limit Is Real

SEC's official policy is clear: requests must include a contact User-Agent header and stay under 10 requests per second. That sounds generous until you're scanning 200 tickers — one submissions JSON per company, then XML downloads for any new filings.

Data Acquisition Pipeline

Step 1 Download company_tickers.json, build ticker → CIK map. Cached 24 hours — single download per deployment.
Step 2 For each SP100 + large-cap stock (~200 tickers), fetch submissions/{CIK}.json and find Form 4 filings from the last 2 days.
Step 3 Download and parse Form 4 XML. Extract only nonDerivativeTransaction records — derivative tables are skipped entirely.
Step 4 Two-layer cleaning → UPSERT to insider_transactions table with 4-field composite unique key for idempotent deduplication.
Step 5 5-day lookback aggregation for cluster detection → signal tiering → UPSERT to event_signals table.

The pipeline runs with Semaphore(5) + 0.12s per-request delay, keeping safely within EDGAR's rate limit. Scanning ~200 tickers typically completes in 10–20 minutes — well within the post-close window when the job fires at 10:05 NZT (65 minutes after US market close).

Limitations: What We're Not Hiding

US equities only

SEC EDGAR covers US-listed companies. No equivalent free structured data exists for Hong Kong, Taiwan, Singapore, Japan, or Australia.

Up to T+2 reporting lag

Insiders have two business days to file. A significant purchase on Monday might not appear in EDGAR until Wednesday. This is not a real-time signal.

Correlation, not causation

The historical excess returns are statistical associations. Insiders make mistakes. They buy at bad times. This is a probabilistic edge, not a guarantee.

Not yet backtested on the StockQueen universe

We rely on academic literature for the historical statistics. The actual predictive performance of insider cluster buys within our specific stock pool is a validation we haven't completed yet. That's next.
