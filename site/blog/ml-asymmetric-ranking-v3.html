ML-V3A: How Asymmetric Labels Doubled Our 2024 Returns and Halved Drawdown

March 20, 2026 • 12 min read • StockQueen Research Team

ML-V2 was a disappointment. It improved Sharpe by +0.14 in crash environments but systematically dragged down returns by −7% in bull markets—exactly when an offensive strategy should be pressing its advantage. After diagnosing the root cause, we made one targeted fix. ML-V3A achieved +104.4% return in 2024 (vs +46.4% for the baseline) with a max drawdown of just −8.1% (vs −40.3%). Here's exactly what we changed and why it worked.

The ML Layer: What It Does

StockQueen's core engine scores every stock in our 500-ticker universe using a 22-dimensional factor vector—momentum windows, volume trends, relative strength, fundamentals, and market structure signals. The top-N stocks by score are held each week.

The ML layer sits on top of this. After the factor scores are computed, an XGBoost ranking model re-orders the top-10 candidates and selects the final 6 positions. It's a re-ranking filter, not a replacement for factor scoring. The key question is: does the model learn anything genuinely useful about which of the top-10 factor picks will outperform in the next week?

How the ML Pipeline Works

1. Factor engine scores all 500 tickers → selects top-10 candidates
2. XGBoost ranker takes the top-10's 22-feature vectors as input
3. Model outputs a re-ranked score for each candidate
4. Final portfolio = top-6 by ML score (not original factor score)
5. Model retrains monthly on 18 months of rolling data (every 1st of the month)

Why ML-V2 Failed in Bull Markets

ML-V2 used symmetric cross-sectional z-scores as training labels. For each stock in the training data, we computed its weekly return, normalized it to a z-score against the universe that week, and fed that to the XGBoost ranker.

The problem: with 22 features all weighted roughly equally (~0.050 importance each), the model treated an "avoid a loser" signal the same as an "press a winner" signal. In a bull market, where the upside is asymmetric and the real alpha comes from maximally loading onto momentum leaders, this symmetric treatment systematically diluted the portfolio's best ideas.

ML-V2 Root Cause: Symmetric Label Problem

V2 Symmetric Labels

z_score = (return - mean) / std

A +2.0 z-score winner and a −2.0 z-score loser receive equal but opposite weight in the loss function. The model learns equally to avoid losers and to press winners—but in bull markets, avoiding losers barely matters. What matters is conviction on the top picks.

V3A Asymmetric Labels

z = z_raw × 1.5  if z_raw > 0
z = z_raw × 0.5  if z_raw < 0

Positive signals are amplified 1.5×. Negative signals are dampened to 0.5×. The model is forced to learn "what makes a winner exceptional" rather than balancing winners and losers equally.

The asymmetric label is a deliberate inductive bias. We're telling the model: "Being wrong about a loser costs half as much as being right about a winner." This pushes the learned weights toward momentum amplification features, which is exactly what you want in an offensive rotation strategy.

W5 2024: The Defining Test

Window 5 (2024 AI bull market) is the most important validation point. The baseline had a 40.3% max drawdown with a mediocre Sharpe of 0.50—a classic "made money but barely slept" scenario. V3A compressed the drawdown to 8.1% while more than doubling the return to 104.4%.

This isn't a coincidence. In a trending AI bull market, the asymmetric label pushes the model to concentrate in the stocks already exhibiting the strongest momentum signals. Meanwhile, the dampened negative labels reduce the model's incentive to rotate out of current winners prematurely.

Honest Caveat: W2 2021

V3A fell behind baseline in W2 (2021 peak bull): +174.8% vs +235.0%. This is worth explaining, not hiding.

The 2021 baseline achieved +235% because our dynamic universe—with over 500 momentum-screened tickers—naturally captured the meme stock and speculative growth surge. In a parabolic environment like that, any aggressive momentum strategy with concentrated positions performs exceptionally. The ML model, by design, applies selectivity that in extreme conditions can sacrifice raw upside for better risk-adjusted performance.

We view this as acceptable: V3A still returned +174.8% in that window and improved Sharpe (3.48 → 3.61). The strategy doesn't need to catch every speculative surge to generate lasting alpha.

Monthly Auto-Retraining

A static ML model trained on 2020–2024 data would gradually drift as market conditions evolve. We solve this with a rolling 18-month retraining window that runs automatically on the 1st of every month.

Retraining Pipeline

1. Fetch 18 months of weekly OHLCV data (AV API, ~7 min)
2. Run rotation backtest to collect weekly scoring snapshots
3. Build training data with asymmetric z-score labels
4. Retrain XGBRanker → save model → hot-reload in memory
5. Feishu notification (feature importance, rank correlation)

Why 18 months? It covers at least one full market regime cycle while keeping AV API costs manageable. Full historical retraining would take 30+ minutes; 18 months takes ~7 minutes.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Asymmetric labels are a simple, targeted fix. One line of code (z × 1.5 for positives, z × 0.5 for negatives) delivered a 6× improvement in Sharpe lift over V2.
  • ✓ The dynamic universe matters as much as the model. V3A on the 500-ticker dynamic pool consistently outperforms V2 on the 200-ticker static pool—more training data directly translates to a better-calibrated ranker.
  • ✓ ML is a re-ranking enhancement, not a standalone signal. It works because the underlying factor scores are already high-quality. Poor factor scores would produce poor ML candidates regardless of the label design.
  • ⚠ The 2024 result is strong evidence but not guaranteed to repeat. All results are from controlled A/B walk-forward tests on unseen windows. Past performance does not guarantee future results.
